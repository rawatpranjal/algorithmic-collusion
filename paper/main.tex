\title{On the Design of Auctions when Algorithms Learn to Bid}
\author{Pranjal Rawat}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Algorithms increasingly automate bidding in online auctions, raising concerns about tacit collusion. Prior work shows that reinforcement-learning agents can spontaneously converge to bid suppression, but it remains unclear which algorithmic and market design factors drive this phenomenon. This paper reports four controlled experiments, spanning Q-learning, contextual bandits, and budget-constrained pacing, that use factorial designs to isolate the effects of auction format, learning parameters, competitive pressure, and budget constraints on seller revenue. Across all settings, first-price auctions systematically underperform second-price auctions, with revenue losses driven by strategic bid shading rather than algorithmic artifacts. Collusion is amplified by fewer bidders, inefficient exploration, and tighter budgets, while reserve prices and second-price mechanisms provide robust mitigation. These results suggest that advertising exchanges relying on first-price auctions may be systematically vulnerable to coordinated bid suppression.
\end{abstract}
