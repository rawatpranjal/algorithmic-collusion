\section{Introduction}

Intelligent algorithms are rapidly taking over real-time computerized auctions and markets in areas such as online marketplaces for pre-owned items, display advertising, sponsored search, financial trading, electricity, transportation, and public procurement. Significant concerns arise about the efficiency of auctions—originally tailored to human participants—under this new regime of algorithmic bidding. For instance, in large-scale advertising exchanges like Google AdSense, even minor inefficiencies or bid suppression can cause million-dollar losses. Recent work on algorithmic bidding in auctions has established that reinforcement-learning agents can spontaneously converge to sub-competitive bidding patterns, whether through strategic bid shading, failure to converge to equilibrium, or budget-mediated rationing. Calvano et al. (2020) demonstrate this phenomenon in pricing games, while Klein (2021) extends the analysis to sequential pricing with Q-learning. Banchio and Skrzypacz (2022) examine first- versus second-price auctions, finding that the former is more prone to bid suppression under simple Q-learning. However, these studies focus on narrow settings (constant valuations, small numbers of bidders, simple exploration mechanisms), as reviewed in Section~\ref{sec:literature}, and do not systematically parse how algorithmic factors (learning rates, discount factors, synchronization modes, budget constraints) amplify or mitigate bid suppression and revenue shortfalls. Empirical data on real-world algorithmic auctions are fairly inaccessible, as most auctions are proprietary, and theoretical work faces similar challenges: modelling high-dimensional dynamic systems of Q-learning agents is analytically intractable, and existing theoretical papers typically focus on small state/action spaces and crude learning algorithms.

This paper adopts a rigorous statistical approach and documents important facts about algorithmic learning in repeated auctions. A parallel literature examines budget-constrained pacing in online advertising auctions (Aggarwal et al., 2019; Balseiro and Gur, 2019; Conitzer et al., 2022; Gaitonde et al., 2023), focusing on efficiency and convergence rather than bid suppression; Section~\ref{sec:literature} reviews this body of work in detail. The literature uses ``algorithmic collusion'' to encompass any learning-driven revenue shortfall relative to competitive benchmarks (Calvano et al., 2020; Banchio \& Skrzypacz, 2022). We adopt this convention but emphasise that our focus is squarely on seller revenue outcomes under algorithmic learning. We do not investigate whether agents develop reward-punishment strategies such as grim trigger or tit-for-tat, nor do we attempt to distinguish genuine tacit collusion from suboptimal convergence or unintended algorithmic synchronisation. The relevant question for auction design is not whether revenue shortfalls constitute ``collusion'' in the game-theoretic sense, but which design parameters most effectively prevent them. This paper addresses the gap by conducting a fully randomized factorial experiment comprising four experiments (six sub-experiments), each involving hundreds of independent trials and up to 100,000 simulated auctions per trial. Bidders apply reinforcement learning or bandit-based exploration under varied institutional elements (e.g., private vs.\ affiliated values, number of bidders, reserve prices) and algorithmic parameters (e.g., discount factors, Q-learning rates, synchronous vs.\ asynchronous updates, different exploration rules).

Prior work on learning-driven bid suppression identifies individual mechanisms in isolation: Calvano et al.\ (2020) study discount factors, Banchio and Skrzypacz (2022) compare auction formats, and Hansen et al.\ (2021) examine the number of competitors. This paper compares all of these factors simultaneously within a unified factorial framework, establishing a hierarchy of relative importance rather than cataloguing individual effects. The experiments span Q-learning, contextual bandits (LinUCB and Thompson Sampling, analysed separately), and budget-constrained pacing algorithms, incorporate affiliated values to capture the continuum between private and common values, and measure revenue, price volatility, no-sale rates, and winner identity patterns. By varying auction format, the number of bidders, reserve prices, exploration schemes, and budget constraints in orthogonal factorial designs, the analysis cleanly separates main effects from interactions and ranks factors by statistical and economic significance.

Two patterns emerge consistently. First, structural market parameters dominate algorithmic design factors in determining seller outcomes: in unconstrained settings the number of bidders is the strongest driver of revenue, consistent with classical auction theory (Bulow and Klemperer, 1996), while under budget constraints the budget multiplier takes over as the primary determinant. Second, the effect of auction format on revenue is context-dependent: equivalent under Q-learning with constant valuations, lower for first-price under contextual bandits, and higher for first-price under budget-constrained pacing. Algorithmic design choices interact with auction format in distinct ways, demonstrating that mechanism design must account for the prevailing bidding technology. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesises findings across all experiments.
