\section{Discussion and Generalisability}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Competitive Pressure and Budget Constraints as Primary Determinants}

The most robust empirical finding across all experiments is that structural market parameters dominate algorithmic design choices. Table~\ref{tab:cross_exp_factors} ranks the top factors by absolute $t$-statistic for revenue in each experiment. In unconstrained settings (Experiments~1--3), the number of bidders yields the largest effect, with magnitudes consistently larger than auction format. Under budget constraints (Experiments~4a--4b), the budget multiplier dominates, with the number of bidders ranking second to fourth, reflecting the binding role of budget tightness in pacing environments.

The dominance of market thickness extends classical auction theory to the RL setting: competitive pressure has long been understood as the primary driver of revenue in static equilibria (Bulow and Klemperer, 1996; Vickrey, 1961), and the factorial analysis confirms this ranking persists when bidders are learning algorithms. In unconstrained experiments, adding bidders dominates revenue with margins over the next-ranked factor of \ExpOneRevTopMarginRatio-fold (Experiment~1) to \ExpThreeARevTopMarginRatio-fold (Experiment~3a). In budget-constrained pacing (Experiments~4a--4b), the budget multiplier takes over as the primary revenue driver, reflecting the structural role of budget tightness in autobidding markets.

The direction and magnitude of the effect vary by context: the revenue consequence of auction format depends on the learning algorithm and environment rather than being an inherent property of the payment rule. The measurement horizon also matters. Table~\ref{tab:cross_exp_revenue} reports end-state revenue (final 1{,}000 episodes). When lifetime revenue is used instead, the auction format effect attenuates or reverses: both formats generate above-equilibrium revenue during learning, but the surplus is larger under first-price rules, rendering the two formats statistically indistinguishable over the full trajectory in Experiment~2.\footnote{First-price learning-phase premium: \ExpTwoFPAPremium; second-price: \ExpTwoSPAPremium. Lifetime revenue model: $|t| = \ExpTwoAllRevAuctionAbsT$, $p \ExpTwoAllRevAuctionPFmt$.} The auction format $\times$ number of bidders interaction is significant in Experiments~1 and~3a but in opposite directions. Under Q-learning (Experiment~1), additional bidders reduce the auction format effect, consistent with competitive pressure disciplining bids regardless of the payment rule. Under LinUCB (Experiment~3a), additional bidders worsen first-price performance, consistent with optimism-based exploration amplifying conservative bidding in multi-agent settings.

\subsection{Exploration and Aggressiveness as Moderators}

Across experiments, factors that promote bid commitment improve first-price auction revenue: the discount factor and update mode in Q-learning (Experiment~1) and the bidder objective in dual pacing (Experiment~4a). In PI pacing (Experiment~4b), auction format itself is a strong revenue driver, with first-price rules increasing revenue, and this effect is moderated by budget tightness through a significant auction format $\times$ budget multiplier interaction. LinUCB (Experiment~3a) diverges: higher exploration intensity \emph{reduces} revenue, as optimism-based exploration reinforces conservative bidding rather than destabilising low-bid equilibria. The welfare consequences of algorithmic design choices are algorithm-specific.

Table~\ref{tab:cross_exp_convergence} compares convergence times across all experiments. In unconstrained settings, contextual bandits converge substantially faster than Q-learning. Pacing experiments use a different convergence concept (within-episode budget depletion rather than cross-episode strategy settling), so direct comparison of convergence times between pacing and unconstrained experiments is not meaningful. The key determinant is \emph{which equilibrium} agents converge to, not convergence speed per se.

\subsection{Winner Concentration}

In the LinUCB experiment (Experiment~3a), first-price auctions produce higher winner entropy than second-price auctions, consistent with symmetric bid suppression preserving roughly equitable winner rotation. This pattern does not replicate under Thompson sampling (Experiment~3b), where the auction format effect on winner entropy is not statistically significant, suggesting that the symmetric bid suppression mechanism is specific to optimism-based exploration rather than a general property of contextual bandits. Under Q-learning with affiliated valuations (Experiment~2), the pattern reverses: second-price auctions exhibit marginally higher entropy, though the difference is not statistically significant. The direction of the winner concentration effect thus depends on the learning algorithm, with only LinUCB producing more symmetric outcomes under first-price rules.

\subsection{Factors with Context-Dependent Effects}

Two factors expected to matter based on theory show uneven effects. The affiliation parameter $\eta$, varied across Experiments~2, 3a, and~3b from independent private values to near-common values, shows no significant effect on revenue in Experiment~2 ($p = 0.056$) but becomes significant in both contextual bandit experiments (Experiment~3a, $t = -5.7$, $p < 0.001$; Experiment~3b, $t = -3.2$, $p = 0.002$), indicating that algorithm sophistication moderates sensitivity to value correlation. Reserve prices show algorithm-dependent effects on revenue: significant under Q-learning with constant valuations (Experiment~1, $t = 5.1$, positive) and Thompson sampling (Experiment~3b, $t = -7.9$, negative), but not under LinUCB or pacing. The opposite signs suggest that reserve prices interact with the learning algorithm: they support revenue when bidders shade bids (Q-learning) but reduce it when bidders are already bidding aggressively (Thompson sampling with affiliated values).

\subsection{Key Takeaways}

Competition is the single most important determinant of auction revenue when bidders are learning algorithms. In every unconstrained experiment, increasing the number of bidders produces a larger revenue effect than any other factor, including auction format, learning rate, or exploration strategy. This finding echoes classical auction theory and confirms that it extends to the algorithmic setting.

Auction format effects are context-dependent rather than universal. There is no blanket advantage for either first-price or second-price auctions. The direction and magnitude of the format effect depend on the learning algorithm and environment, shifting from negligible under Q-learning with constant valuations, to favouring second-price under affiliated values, to favouring first-price under budget-constrained pacing.

The degree of value affiliation has no effect on revenue under Q-learning, but becomes a significant driver under contextual bandits. This divergence suggests that Q-learning agents do not exploit the informational structure of affiliated values, whereas bandit algorithms with richer state representations are sensitive to the correlation between bidder signals.

Higher exploration intensity reduces revenue under contextual bandits. In both LinUCB and Thompson sampling experiments, more aggressive exploration leads to lower seller revenue, as optimism-based or sampling-based exploration reinforces conservative bidding strategies rather than disrupting collusive equilibria.

Budget tightness replaces competition as the primary revenue driver under pacing. In autobidding experiments with budget constraints, the budget multiplier dominates all other factors, reflecting the structural role that spending limits play in constraining bidder aggressiveness.

Faster convergence does not imply higher revenue. LinUCB agents converge substantially faster than Q-learning agents but produce lower revenues, because the equilibrium to which they converge matters more than how quickly they reach it.

First-price auctions produce more symmetric winner distributions only under LinUCB, not under Thompson sampling or Q-learning. The bid suppression mechanism that equalises winning probabilities across bidders appears to be specific to optimism-based exploration, not a general property of first-price payment rules.

Reserve prices have opposite effects depending on the learning algorithm. They improve revenue under Q-learning with constant valuations, where bidders shade bids and the reserve provides a floor, but reduce revenue under Thompson sampling with affiliated values, where bidders already bid aggressively and the reserve causes no-sale outcomes.

Higher discount factors suppress revenue under Q-learning. Patient agents that weight future rewards more heavily sustain lower-bid collusive equilibria for longer, resulting in the discount factor ranking as the second most important revenue driver in the baseline Q-learning experiment.

\subsection{External Validity}

The qualitative patterns documented here are likely to hold in settings characterised by repeated interactions, autonomous learning, and sealed-bid formats, conditions that closely approximate programmatic advertising markets. External validity to specific real-world markets requires empirical validation with proprietary auction data. Settings involving few-shot auctions, human bidders, explicit coordination, or combinatorial formats may produce qualitatively different outcomes.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The most effective lever available to sellers is ensuring sufficient competition: in unconstrained settings, increasing the number of bidders improves revenue more than any other intervention tested, including switching auction formats. In budget-constrained environments, relaxing budget tightness has the largest impact. The effect of auction format is context-dependent, so sellers should choose format based on the prevailing bidding technology rather than adopting a blanket preference. For platforms committed to first-price formats, structural interventions such as binding budget constraints or lower discount factors can improve revenue regardless of the specific learning algorithm.
