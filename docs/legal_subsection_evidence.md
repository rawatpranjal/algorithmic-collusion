## Chain of Argument (Plain English)

### 1. The Problem (Agreement Gap)

Current antitrust law was designed around human conspiracies. To prosecute price-fixing, regulators need evidence that firms reached an "agreement" -- a meeting of minds, secret communications, a handshake. When pricing algorithms independently learn to charge high prices without ever talking to each other, there is no agreement to find. Mehra calls this the "agreement gap." The OECD warned in 2017 and 2023 that algorithms make tacit coordination easier by increasing transparency, enabling instant retaliation, and removing the human temptation to cheat. Despite all this concern, no country has successfully prosecuted purely autonomous algorithmic tacit collusion. The one area where enforcement has progressed -- shared pricing software like RealPage -- involves explicit coordination through a common platform, which is legally distinct.

### 2. What People Propose (Remedies)

Scholars have proposed several ways to close the enforcement gap. Harrington wants a per se rule banning algorithms with collusion-facilitating properties, shifting the focus from what happened to how the algorithm was designed. Hartline and coauthors propose a statistical audit: test whether an algorithm's pricing data is consistent with competitive behavior. Gal argues that algorithms are actually easier to regulate than humans because you can read the code. Zhang proposes a market-design approach: inject calibrated noise into the data that algorithms observe to disrupt coordination. Various jurisdictions have started legislative responses. But Crane warns that AI may be so transformative that incremental legal reform will not suffice, while Petit and Schwalbe caution against premature regulation.

### 3. Why the Evidence Is Shaky (Technology-Specificity)

Nearly all the simulation evidence comes from Q-learning experiments. Even within Q-learning, the results are fragile: Bichler et al. show collusion disappears with different hyperparameters or when Q-learners face other algorithm types. Douglas proves that collusion is a deterministic-algorithm phenomenon -- persistent randomness prevents it. The distinction between "genuine" collusion (with learned punishment) and "spurious" collusion (an artifact of limited exploration) is contested. Most importantly, the algorithms studied bear little resemblance to what real advertising platforms use. Real platforms run budget-pacing algorithms, not Q-learning. Nobody has tested whether pacing produces the same patterns.

### 4. What We Contribute

We sidestep the legal question entirely. A revenue shortfall hurts the seller regardless of its cause. We test both learning algorithms and pacing algorithms in a common factorial framework. Our key finding: the auction-format effect reverses sign between the two algorithm families, so format recommendations from Q-learning studies do not generalize to pacing. Structural market parameters (especially the number of bidders) consistently dominate format choice. The methodology provides transparent, reproducible evidence.
